When I interviewed for an AI Lead Engineer position, I was told that it’s impossible to build anything truly worthwhile using Chinese LLMs.

Well… challenge accepted.

Especially considering that the prices of Western frontier models keep going up while subscription plans keep offering less and less. And honestly, I have little doubt that within a year most serious AI products will either be paying directly for tokens or running self-hosted models.

So I decided to build something of my own: a swarm of expert agents that research topics on the web, validate information, and use an LLM as a judge to evaluate the quality of the final result.

The platform is built with LangGraph and consists of five specialized agents:

* Planner generates a research plan.
* Searcher gathers information using multiple search providers with automatic fallback.
* Fact Checker validates findings and stores unique facts in Qdrant.
* Summarizer compiles structured reports.
* Judge evaluates report quality using a formal scoring rubric and determines whether additional research is required.

One of the key features is a vector memory layer built on Qdrant and Ollama embeddings. It provides semantic deduplication, cross-iteration knowledge accumulation, and prevents reprocessing information that has already been discovered, reducing LLM costs and improving efficiency.

When a report fails to meet the quality threshold, the system doesn’t simply rerun the entire workflow. Instead, it performs targeted follow-up research focused only on the missing topics identified by the Judge.

The project also includes an agentic News Sender that automatically researches a given topic and delivers reports via email, Telegram, or Discord.

And of course, the whole thing is containerized with Docker.

For development, I used Hermes Agent powered by Step-3.7-Flash as a technical lead and reviewer. Hermes generated tasks for DeepSeek-4-Pro, which acted as the implementation engineer, wrote code, and executed tests.

Hermes was also connected to Langfuse via MCP, allowing it to observe how Gemini-3.1-Flash-Light interacted with itself through LangGraph and Qdrant. Based on those observations and our discussions, it continuously refined the tasks assigned to DeepSeek.

The codebase itself was opened in Cursor, but I mostly used it as a code editor. Apart from some autocomplete and a few typo fixes, the longest thing I personally wrote in Cursor was this text.

To evaluate output quality, I used the Gemini desktop application on macOS. I would submit the same research queries, compare Gemini’s summaries against the reports generated by my system, and analyze the differences. I also occasionally consulted Gemini and ChatGPT on architectural questions related to expert systems. Neither of them touched the code.

For testing, I repeatedly ran the system against a variety of topics:

1. The present and future of agentic AI development (because of course I did).
2. Funny squirrel stories (to test retrieval of low-value and potentially noisy information).
3. Regulation of new pharmaceuticals in the AI era, where regulators struggle to keep pace with research labs.
4. How governments maintain sovereignty over algorithms involved in cryptocurrency and traditional financial markets in an age of autonomous agents.
5. At what point in 2010 it became clear that moles had initiated the downfall of European civilization (a deliberate hallucination test; the swarm honestly reported that it found no supporting evidence after the first research pass).
6. How AI, TikTok, and modern drug culture are changing the music industry (the swarm correctly concluded that AI and TikTok are significant factors, while the premise regarding “new drugs” was unsupported).

The result is a research swarm consisting of five specialized agents delivered as a service, and a reinforced belief that Chinese models are absolutely capable of building serious systems when placed inside a well-designed architecture.
